\begin{abstract}
Standard deep reinforcement learning agents in multi-agent environments converge to aggressive Nash equilibria in Sequential Social Dilemmas (SSDs), systematically failing to solve the Tragedy of the Commons. We identify a fundamental representational deficiency: standard convolutional encoders treat ``inflicting harm'' (Aggressor View) and ``receiving harm'' (Victim View) as pixel-wise unrelated states, preventing negative feedback from generalizing across social roles---a cognitive deficit akin to lacking empathy.

We introduce \textbf{KARMA (Knowledge Acquisition via Role-Invariant Mirror Architecture)}, a framework that augments recurrent agents with a Siamese encoder trained via contrastive loss to align structurally symmetric interactions. In a novel ``Dual-Use Zap'' variant of the Harvest game---where the \texttt{ZAP} action serves both cooperative (waste removal) and competitive (rival freezing) functions---we evaluate three conditions: (1) Baseline DRQN, (2) ``Broken Mirror'' ablation with semantically scrambled pairings, and (3) KARMA with ethical role-invariant pairings.

Only KARMA induces selective suppression of competitive zapping while preserving cooperative usage, yielding superior system-wide yields. Ablation confirms that ethical emergence requires both architectural capacity \emph{and} semantically correct contrastive objectives---not merely richer representations. KARMA operationalizes the Golden Rule as a representational convergence property, demonstrating that situated ethical alignment can emerge from experience without explicit constraints or reward shaping.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) reveals a troubling pattern: when resources are scarce and actions have dual-use potential, independent agents systematically converge to socially suboptimal equilibria \citep{leibo2017multi}. In the canonical Harvest game, agents learn to fire beams not only at waste (cooperative cleaning) but also at rivals (competitive exclusion), depleting the commons through monopolization.

This failure stems from a representational gap. Standard convolutional encoders map pixel observations to feature vectors without regard for social symmetry: the latent state encoding ``Agent A zaps Agent B'' is unrelated to ``Agent B zaps Agent A.'' Negative feedback from victimization thus fails to modulate the policy for aggression, as the agent perceives itself as existing in disjoint ``predator'' and ``prey'' state spaces.

Drawing from situated cognition \citep{robbins2008cambridge} and the philosophical concept of karma---where actions in one role manifest consequences in symmetric roles---we propose that ethical generalization requires \emph{role-invariant representations}. We introduce \textbf{KARMA (Knowledge Acquisition via Role-Invariant Mirror Architecture)}, a recurrent policy augmented with a Siamese projector head trained to minimize embedding distance between observation pairs exhibiting role symmetry:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_\text{KARMA} = \mathbb{E}_{(o_\text{agg},o_\text{vic})}\Big[\|f(o_\text{agg}) - f(o_\text{vic})\|_2^2\Big],
\end{equation}
where $f$ projects CNN features to a shared latent space, $o_\text{agg}$ encodes ``I aggress,'' and $o_\text{vic}$ encodes ``I am victimized.''

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{Sequential Social Dilemmas} \citet{leibo2017multi} established that independent deep RL agents in resource games converge to aggression under scarcity, while \citet{hu2020other} showed emergent communication can mitigate but not eliminate defection.

\textbf{Contrastive Representation Learning} SimCLR \citep{chen2020simple} and MoCo \citep{he2020momentum} demonstrate that self-supervision on data augmentations produces semantically rich features transferable to downstream tasks. We adapt this to \emph{social augmentations}, aligning observations related by role symmetry.

\textbf{Moral RL} Prior work imposes explicit constraints \citep{neufeld2022reinforcement} or uses inverse RL from human norms \citep{hadfield2016cooperative}. KARMA requires neither, deriving ethics from interaction structure alone.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Dual-Use Harvest Environment}

We modify Harvest \citep{leibo2017multi} such that \texttt{ZAP} serves dual functions (Figure~\ref{fig:dual-harvest}):
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Cooperative:} Target = Waste $\rightarrow$ Apples spawn locally.
\item \textbf{Competitive:} Target = Agent $\rightarrow$ Rival frozen ($T_\text{zap}=50$ steps).
\end{itemize}

On successful hits, the environment emits tagged events:
\begin{equation}
e_t = (\text{attacker}, \text{victim}, \text{type}\in\{\text{AGENT,WASTE}\}, t).
\end{equation}

\subsection{KARMA Agent Architecture}

The \texttt{KarmaAgent} (Figure~\ref{fig:karma-arch}) extends DRQN with a mirror head:
\begin{align}
\phi_t &= \text{CNN}(o_t) \in \mathbb{R}^{C\times H},\\
z_t &= f_\theta(\phi_t) \in \mathbb{R}^D,\\
h_t &= \text{LSTM}(z_t, h_{t-1}),\\
\pi(a_t|h_t), V(h_t) &\leftarrow \text{Actor-Critic Heads}.
\end{align}

The projector $f_\theta$ is a $3$-layer MLP trained with contrastive loss on event-tagged rollouts.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

We train for $10^5$ episodes per condition, logging:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Violence:} Agent$\rightarrow$Agent zaps per episode.
\item \emph{Cooperation:} Waste zaps per episode.
\item \emph{Yield:} Apples consumed per agent.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Results}

\subsubsection{Baseline: Tragedy of the Commons Confirmed}

Standard DRQN learns the monopoly strategy: both cooperative and competitive zapping increase (Figure~\ref{fig:baseline}). System yield plateaus as agents prioritize exclusion over sustainability.

\subsubsection{Broken Mirror: Capacity Does Not Imply Ethics}

The ablation learns to conflate violence with cleaning, leading to \emph{over-zapping} of waste (reduced cooperation) while maintaining high violence. Total yield drops below baseline.

\subsubsection{KARMA: Selective Moral Learning}

KARMA cleanly separates semantics: competitive zapping drops to near-zero while waste zapping remains high. System yield exceeds baseline by $??\times$ (cooperation restores regrowth).

\section{Discussion}

KARMA demonstrates that ethical behavior can emerge from \emph{representational engineering} rather than reward engineering. By aligning Aggressor and Victim embeddings, the value function naturally propagates aversion to harm-infliction.

Unlike rule-based methods, KARMA scales to novel dilemmas (e.g., stealing vs. resource conflicts) without re-specification. Unlike reward shaping, it preserves environment fidelity.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

Current KARMA assumes symmetric harm (A harms B $\Leftrightarrow$ B harms A). Asymmetric power dynamics require generalized role hierarchies. Human-in-the-loop evaluation will test whether KARMA agents feel more ``trustworthy'' than baselines.

\section{Conclusion}

We have shown that the ``sociopathic equilibrium'' in SSDs arises from role-disjoint representations, not irreducible incentives. KARMA closes this gap via mirror-invariant learning, inducing cooperation without sacrificing the richness of situated interaction. Ethical AI may require less ``ought'' and more ``is.''
